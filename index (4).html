The Rebuild · before you begin

A careful, honest shoulder rebuild.

This is a 90-day, evidence-based rehab program built around one rule: your shoulder sets the pace, not the calendar. It progresses only when the joint earns it — measured by how you feel the morning after, not mid-workout.

Because you've had prior shoulder surgeries and a suspected labral issue, this program is only safe as a complement to real medical care — not a replacement for it. Please confirm:

I've had (or will get) this cleared by my surgeon or physical therapist before starting, and I'll bring them this program.
I understand the red-flag signs (below) and will stop and seek care if they appear, rather than pushing through.

  • A sense the shoulder is slipping or about to pop out
  • Night pain that keeps getting worse over days
  • New weakness, numbness, or tingling down the arm

How this works & staying safe

⚠ Stop & call your provider if you get

  • Sharp pain, catching, locking, or a "dead-arm" feeling
  • A sense the shoulder is slipping or about to dislocate
  • Night pain that keeps worsening over several days
  • New weakness, numbness, or tingling down the arm
  • Pain above 3/10 that hasn't settled by the next morning
Who this program fits

This is built for a chronic, conservatively-managed shoulder — a suspected labral or rotator-cuff irritation being rehabbed, not a fresh injury and not a recent surgical repair still in its protected phase. It is not a fit for a freshly repaired labrum (those follow the surgeon's time-based protocol, which is the opposite of pushing when ready), an acute dislocation, a full cuff tear, or a fracture. Different labral tears (front, back, top) and true instability also need different precautions. If any of that describes his shoulder, have his PT adjust or replace this before he starts. When in doubt, their instruction beats the app.

The rules this program runs on
01

The morning after is the verdict. How the shoulder feels the next day — not during the set — decides whether you hold, progress, or back off. Same-or-better = it tolerated the load. Worse = too much.

02

The 3/10 ceiling. A little discomfort during rehab is fine. Pain past 3 out of 10, or pain that lingers into the next day, means dial it back.

03

Gates, not the calendar. The day counter is a guide. Stay at a level until it's genuinely easy and pain-free before taking the next step — even if that means repeating a week.

04

Load in order. Hold still first (isometrics), then move against light resistance, then heavier and slower. Pressing comes back last, through shoulder-friendly variations before any flat barbell work.

05

A flare isn't failure. If it flares, drop back to the last comfortable level for a few days and rebuild. Research shows people who complete rehab — even after a setback — do well.

06

Rotations get their own ramp. Internal and external rotation are the movements that bother this shoulder, so they start below a normal hold — at "find the angle that doesn't hurt at all" — and climb a 6-step ladder only as each step stays pain-free. They progress on their own, independent of everything else.

Why these exercises

The program is built from the conservative-rehab evidence for labral and rotator-cuff shoulders: correct the scapula and posture, restore posterior-shoulder mobility, and strengthen the rotator cuff through the ranges shown (on EMG studies) to activate it best — side-lying and scapular-plane external rotation, full-can raises, prone Y/T work — then rebuild pressing through a graded, joint-sparing ladder. It deliberately avoids the wide-grip, flared-elbow, full-depth pressing position that stresses the front of the joint.
